This email has a classic buried-ask problem. Sarah has to read to the very last word to find out what you actually need — and even then, the ask is so hedged it barely registers as a request. Here's the side-by-side:

---

**Original**

> Hi Sarah, I hope you're doing well. I wanted to follow up on our last discussion around team capacity. As you know, Q4 has been really intense and the team has been stretched. We've had a few conversations internally about priorities. Given all of this context, I think it might be worth exploring whether we could potentially consider adding a headcount in the data engineering area in the next planning cycle if the budget allows.

---

**BLUF version**

> Subject: Headcount request for data engineering — next planning cycle
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I'd like to request one data engineering headcount in the next planning cycle.
>
> The team has been at capacity through Q4 — we've had to make tradeoffs on priorities, and data engineering is the clearest bottleneck. I'm happy to put together a business case if that would help move it forward. Would you be open to discussing this before planning kicks off?

---

**What changed and why**

- **Subject line added with the actual ask.** The original had no subject line shown, but based on the email it would likely have been something vague like "Team capacity follow-up." A subject line should tell Sarah exactly what this is before she opens it.
- **Ask moved to the first sentence.** Sarah previously had to read through four sentences of context and hedging before reaching the point. The request now leads — everything else is support.
- **Passive hedging removed.** "I think it might be worth exploring whether we could potentially consider adding" is six layers of softening around a direct ask. It doesn't make you sound more polite — it makes you sound uncertain about your own request. The rewrite is direct without being aggressive: "I'd like to request."
- **Context kept, but repositioned.** The Q4 intensity and priority tradeoffs are still there — they're valid support. They just come after the ask, not before it.
- **A clear next step added.** The original ended without telling Sarah what you wanted her to do with this information. The rewrite closes with an offer and a light question, which gives her something to act on.
