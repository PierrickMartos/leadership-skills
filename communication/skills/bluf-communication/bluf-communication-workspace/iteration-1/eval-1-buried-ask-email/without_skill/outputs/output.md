# Email Review

Your email has a real problem: **the actual ask is buried and heavily hedged**, which makes it easy for Sarah to skim past it, deprioritize it, or feel uncertain about what you actually need from her.

Here is a breakdown of the specific issues and a revised version.

---

## What is not working

**1. The ask is buried at the end.**
You spend three sentences on context before getting to what you want. In a skip-level communication, your reader is busy and likely scanning. If the most important thing is the last sentence, there is a real risk it gets missed or treated as an afterthought.

**2. The ask is drowning in hedges.**
Count the qualifiers in your final sentence: "might be worth exploring," "whether we could potentially consider," "if the budget allows." That is four layers of hedging on a single request. This signals either a lack of confidence in the ask or uncertainty about whether you should be making it at all — neither of which serves you well with a skip-level.

**3. The framing is vague.**
"A few conversations internally about priorities" does not give Sarah anything concrete. What were those conversations? What conclusion did you reach? She cannot advocate for you if she does not know what the team has actually decided.

**4. The subject line (implied) and opening are generic.**
"I hope you're doing well" is fine, but opening pleasantries followed by vague context can make the email feel like a soft complaint rather than a clear leadership ask.

---

## Revised version

> **Subject: Headcount request for data engineering — input needed before next planning cycle**
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I want to flag a staffing need I'd like to discuss before the next planning cycle: we need to add a data engineer to the team.
>
> Q4 has put significant strain on our data engineering capacity. We have had to defer [specific work] and are currently at risk of [specific consequence] if we enter the next quarter at the same headcount. We have already deprioritized what we can.
>
> I would like to make a formal headcount request and wanted to align with you first on the right timing and process. Would you have 20 minutes in the next two weeks to discuss?
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

---

## The key principle

**Lead with the ask, follow with the context.** Your skip-level is not going to read your email and wonder "hmm, what does this person need?" — you need to tell them in the first sentence. The context that follows then feels like evidence for a decision already framed, rather than a preamble to something uncertain.

Also, **make the ask concrete and confident.** You do not need to hedge against the budget in the email — that is Sarah's problem to navigate. Your job is to make the need clear so she can advocate for it (or give you a direct no). Hedging the ask makes it easier to dismiss.
