Here's the side-by-side comparison for your report intro.

---

**Original**

> This report presents findings from a three-month analysis of customer churn patterns. We analyzed data from 4,200 accounts across three product tiers using cohort analysis and regression modeling. We collaborated with the CS and sales teams to gather qualitative input. After extensive analysis, we identified several key drivers. The main finding is that customers who don't complete onboarding within 14 days have a 3x higher churn rate in months 2–4.

---

**BLUF version**

> Customers who don't complete onboarding within 14 days churn at 3x the rate of those who do — concentrated in months 2–4. This is the clearest actionable signal from a three-month analysis of 4,200 accounts across three product tiers, using cohort analysis, regression modeling, and qualitative input from CS and sales.

---

**What changed and why**

- **Moved the key finding to the first sentence.** In the original, the most important insight — the 3x churn rate tied to onboarding completion — was buried at the end after four sentences of setup. Leadership reading an intro wants to know what you found, not how you found it. The method can follow the message.
- **Cut the preamble.** "This report presents findings from a three-month analysis..." is a warm-up sentence that restates what is already obvious from the report's existence. Opening with it delays the point and costs the reader's attention before they've got anything useful.
- **Consolidated the methodology into a single supporting sentence.** The sample size, tiers, methods, and stakeholder input are all credibility signals — they belong after the finding, not before it. Grouping them keeps the detail without letting it obscure the lead.
- **Removed "after extensive analysis."** This phrase adds length but no information. Readers assume analysis happened; describing it as "extensive" reads as filler. Cut it.
