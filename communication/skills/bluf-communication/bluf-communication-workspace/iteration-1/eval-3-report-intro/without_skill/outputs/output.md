Your intro has solid content — a clear finding, real data, and a defined scope — but it buries the most important insight at the end. Leadership audiences typically want the key takeaway upfront, not after a methodology walkthrough.

Here's a revised version that leads with the finding:

---

**Revised intro:**

Customers who don't complete onboarding within 14 days churn at 3x the rate during months 2–4. This report explains what's driving that pattern and what we can do about it.

Over the past three months, we analyzed 4,200 accounts across three product tiers using cohort analysis and regression modeling, and gathered qualitative input from the CS and sales teams. The onboarding gap emerged as the single most predictive factor in early churn.

---

**What changed and why:**

1. **Led with the finding.** The 3x churn stat is the most actionable insight in your report. Putting it first signals to leadership that you have a concrete answer, not just a study.

2. **Cut the process narration.** Phrases like "after extensive analysis, we identified several key drivers" add length without adding information. The finding speaks for itself.

3. **Tightened the methods.** The methodology is still there — cohort analysis, regression, cross-functional input — but compressed so it doesn't delay the payoff.

4. **Added a forward hook.** "What we can do about it" signals the report includes recommendations, which is what leadership typically cares most about. Adjust if your report is purely diagnostic.

**One question to consider:** Is the onboarding finding truly the single most important takeaway, or are there two or three findings of roughly equal weight? If so, you could open with a brief list of top findings before diving into each.
