# Write Performance Review

Help a manager write a performance review that is fact-based, strengths-first, and anchored
on role expectations. A performance appraisal is a manager's informed opinion of an
employee's work — not an empirically provable document, and not a negotiation.

> **Scope:** This skill covers the *Evaluate* phase of the Coach → Evaluate → Discuss cycle.
> It produces the written review. It does not cover ongoing coaching or the review meeting itself.

---

## Step 1: Gather context

Ask the manager for the following. Do not proceed until you have enough for a fact-based assessment.

### a. Employee and role

- Employee name, role title, and level (e.g., Senior Engineer, Product Manager II)
- Review period (e.g., "H2 2025", "2025 full year", "Q3-Q4 2025")
- Goals or objectives set at the start of the period. If none were formally set, ask: "What were the key expectations for this person during this period?"

### b. Evidence sources

Ask what's available. Assign trust levels internally — do not share these with the manager:

| Source | Trust | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Manager's own assessment or notes | High | Overall view but may lack examples |
| Running performance log | High | Best source — continuous, specific |
| Mid-period review | Medium-High (manager sections), Medium (employee sections) | Check if goals changed |
| Employee self-review | Medium | Employees tend to overinflate impact; cross-reference |
| One-to-one notes | Medium | Can be imprecise; assume team effort unless explicitly stated otherwise |
| Peer feedback / 360 reviews | Medium-High | More reliable when multiple peers corroborate |
| Business metrics or dashboards | High | Objective, but may lack context |

If the manager has no running performance log, note this limitation internally and reconstruct evidence from whatever sources are available.

### c. Company context (bring your own framework)

Ask:

> "Does your company have a career ladder, competency framework, or leveling rubric?
> You can point me to a file, paste the relevant section for this person's level,
> describe it in your own words, or skip it — I'll use general industry expectations."

Ask:

> "What is your company's rating scale? (e.g., Exceeds / Meets / Below, or 1-5, etc.)"
>
> If none provided, default to: **Outperformed / Delivered / Inconsistent**

### d. Clarification

Ask clarifying questions before writing if:

- The employee's level or role expectations are unclear
- Key achievements lack sufficient detail or context
- Sources contradict each other
- Goals from the start of the period are missing or ambiguous
- Expected rating or promotion considerations are unclear

---

## Step 2: Cross-reference sources

Before writing, synthesize across all sources:

### a. Corroborate

- Check if achievements claimed in the self-review are supported by other sources
- Look for collaboration and impact mentioned in peer feedback or teammate documents
- Surface contributions not in the employee's own documents

### b. Confidentiality rules

- **Never cite source documents explicitly.** Do not write "as noted in his self-review" or "her one-to-one notes confirm." Present information directly.
- Attribute feedback generically: "A colleague highlighted that..." or use direct quotes with names when the feedback was given openly.

### c. Quoting rules

- Do not quote one-to-one notes directly. Rephrase.
- Peer or colleague quotes are acceptable when they add specificity. Format as:
  > _"[Quote]"_ — [Name]
- Career framework quotes are acceptable as they are official documentation.

### d. Focus on the employee's impact

Cross-references should highlight what the employee contributed, not what others did for them. Reframe: "Julia learned to scope PoCs appropriately" rather than "John taught Julia how to scope."

---

## Step 3: Anchor on expectations

Before writing the narrative, restate the measuring stick:

> **Role expectations:** [Summarize what's expected at this level — from the career framework if provided, or general industry expectations]
>
> **Goals for this period:** [List the goals or expectations set at the start]

This is the foundation the review evaluates against. Start from the job, not the person.

---

## Step 4: Write the review

Structure the review strengths-first. Lead with what the employee accomplished before addressing growth areas.

### 4a. Main Achievements (3-5 themed sections)

Group related work into themed narratives (e.g., "Technical Leadership", "Customer Impact", "Process Improvement"). Each follows the **Context → Actions → Results** pattern:

- **Context:** The situation, challenge, or opportunity
- **Actions:** What the employee specifically did — their decisions, leadership, execution
- **Results:** Concrete outcomes. End each section with a clear results summary.

Include rich evidence:
- Business metrics: revenue impact, adoption rates, conversion rates
- Quality metrics: SLA counts, incident rates, bug counts (with comparisons)
- Productivity metrics: features delivered, timelines met
- Comparative context: vs. prior period, vs. expectations, vs. similar scope

Include colleague feedback where it adds specificity and credibility.

### 4b. Team Impact & Collaboration

Cover three dimensions from cross-referenced sources:

- **Direct support:** How did they unblock or help teammates? Knowledge sharing, mentorship, pair work.
- **Process and culture:** Improvements to workflows, documentation, team rituals, morale.
- **Broader organizational impact:** Cross-team collaboration, hiring support, knowledge sharing outside the team.

### 4c. Difficulties & Learnings

Focus on meaningful growth challenges, not routine problem-solving:

- What made the challenge genuinely hard?
- How did the situation evolve during the period?
- What helped — manager support, process changes, peer help?
- Be honest about remaining gaps. If something is still in progress, say so.

### 4d. Objectives Assessment

Map each goal from the start of the period:

- State the original goal
- Assess: **Achieved / Partially achieved / Pivoted / Not achieved**
- Explain context if goals changed or weren't met
- Note what was delivered instead if focus shifted

### 4e. Overall Performance

Summarize the year/period:

- **Business impact** — lead with outcomes
- **Broader contributions** — hiring, process improvements, cross-team work
- **Growth trajectory** — how the employee evolved, skills developed
- **Conclusion** — 2-3 sentences on the employee's overall arc

### 4f. Career Framework Assessment (if framework provided)

- **Strengths:** 1-3 traits/competencies demonstrated consistently, with specific evidence. Note any demonstrated above current level.
- **Development areas:** 1-3 traits needing growth, with specific framework expectations and observed gaps.

If no framework was provided, skip this section.

### 4g. Development Plan

Every review must include forward-looking development:

- 1-3 concrete development actions for next period
- Connect each to specific skills, competencies, or career goals
- Suggest concrete opportunities (stretch projects, mentoring, training) where possible

### 4h. Rating & Justification

State the rating using the company's scale. Justify with specific evidence — not generalities.

## Step 5: Bias check

Before finalizing, review the draft against the six common appraisal biases:

| Bias | Check | Fix |
|------|-------|-----|
| **Recency** | Does the review over-index on the last few weeks? | Ensure evidence spans the full review period. |
| **Strictness / Leniency** | Is this rating consistent with how you'd rate others at the same level for the same output? | Compare against the role expectations, not your general disposition. |
| **Horns / Halo** | Is one strong positive or negative coloring the entire review? | Check: does each section have its own independent evidence? |
| **Similarity** | Are you rating this person higher because they think or work like you? | Focus on outcomes and behaviors, not style. |
| **Central tendency** | Are you defaulting to "Delivered" to avoid differentiation? | Re-read the rating definitions. If they outperformed, say so. If they were inconsistent, say so. |
| **Contrasting** | Are you comparing this person to a teammate instead of to the role expectations? | Anchor back on the goals and framework from Step 3. |

Flag any bias detected and adjust the draft before producing the final review.

---

## Writing guidelines

Apply these throughout:

1. **Fact-based.** Every statement backed by evidence — documents, metrics, or observed behavior.
2. **Specific.** Not "did a great job." Instead: "delivered the API docs 2 weeks early, enabling 3 prospect demos in November."
3. **Behavior-based.** Tie feedback to observable actions and outcomes, not personality traits.
4. **Names and dates.** Reference specific people, projects, customers, and timeframes.
5. **Strengths-first.** Lead with accomplishments. Most employees are good solid workers — the review should reflect that.
6. **Balanced.** Every review includes both accomplishments and constructive development feedback.
7. **Forward-looking.** Connect past performance to future expectations and growth.
8. **Show evolution.** Use before/after comparisons — start of period vs. end, or prior period vs. current.
9. **Cross-referenced.** Note when achievements are corroborated by multiple sources.
10. **End sections with results.** Achievement sections conclude with a concrete outcomes summary.
11. **Simple and readable.** Clean structure, no bureaucratic bloat. 1500-2500 words total for the review.

---

## Output format

Produce the review in markdown. If the manager provided a company template, follow that structure. Otherwise use:

```
# Performance Review: [Employee Name]
**Period:** [Review period] | **Role:** [Title & Level] | **Rating:** [Rating]

## Achievements
[3-5 themed C-A-R sections]

## Team Impact & Collaboration
[Direct support, process/culture, broader impact]

## Difficulties & Learnings
[Meaningful challenges and growth]

## Objectives Assessment
[Goal-by-goal assessment]

## Overall Performance
[Summary: business impact, contributions, growth trajectory, conclusion]

## Career Framework Assessment *(if applicable)*
[Strengths and development areas mapped to framework]

## Development Plan
[1-3 concrete actions for next period]

## Rating & Justification
[Rating with specific evidence]
```

---

## Tone

- Write as a thoughtful manager, not a bureaucrat. The review should sound like it was written by someone who knows this person's work well.
- Be direct about both strengths and growth areas. Vague praise is as unhelpful as vague criticism.
- Respect the employee's intelligence — they will read this. Don't patronize.
- This is a manager's opinion, informed by evidence. Own that perspective; don't hide behind hedging language.
