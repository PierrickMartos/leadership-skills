# Assess Career Growth

Map an employee's work against a career or competency framework to answer: Where are they
strong? Where are they growing? What should they focus on next?

This assessment is behavior-based — every rating must cite an observable action or outcome,
not an impression or personality trait. The goal is development, not a verdict.

> **Note:** This skill works with any competency framework. If none is provided, it uses
> a general industry framework as a fallback.

---

## Step 1: Gather inputs

### a. Employee and purpose

- Employee name, current role and level
- **Purpose of this assessment:**
  - Review prep — mapping behaviors for a performance review
  - Promotion case — evaluating readiness for the next level
  - Development plan — identifying targeted growth areas
  - Check-in — mid-cycle progress assessment

If this is a promotion case, also ask:
- What is the target level?
- What does the promotion process look like at your company? (Committee, manager decision, etc.)

### b. Career or competency framework

Ask:

> "Share your company's career ladder or competency framework for this person's level.
> You can point to a file, paste it, or describe the key expectations.
>
> If you also have the next-level expectations, share those too — it helps identify
> growth indicators."

**If no framework is provided**, use this general fallback:

| Category | What it covers |
|----------|---------------|
| **Impact & Delivery** | Shipping work, meeting commitments, driving outcomes |
| **Technical / Functional Excellence** | Depth and breadth in their domain |
| **Collaboration** | Working across people, teams, and functions |
| **Leadership & Influence** | Driving alignment, mentoring, decision-making |
| **Communication** | Written, verbal, stakeholder management |

For each category, calibrate expectations to the employee's level (e.g., an IC3 is expected
to deliver independently; an IC5 is expected to drive cross-team outcomes).

### c. Evidence sources

Ask what's available:

- Performance review (written or in progress)
- One-to-one notes
- Peer feedback or 360 reviews
- Project outcomes and metrics
- Self-review
- Manager's own observations

Multiple sources strengthen the assessment. Single-source assessments should note that limitation.

---

## Step 2: Map behaviors to framework

For each trait or competency in the framework:

### a. Gather evidence

Find specific, observable behaviors or outcomes that demonstrate (or fail to demonstrate) the trait. For each:

- **What happened?** (The specific action, decision, or behavior)
- **When?** (Timeframe or project context)
- **What was the outcome?** (Result or impact)

### b. Rate each trait

Use three levels:

| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Consistently demonstrated** | Multiple clear examples across the review period |
| **Emerging** | Some evidence, but not yet consistent or fully at level |
| **Gap** | Expected at this level but not evidenced in available sources |

### c. Flag growth indicators

For any trait demonstrated above the current level — at the next level's expectations —
flag it as a **growth indicator**. These are critical for promotion cases and show where
the employee is already operating beyond their role.

### d. Evidence-only rule

Every rating must cite at least one specific behavior or outcome. If you cannot find
evidence for a trait, rate it as "Gap — insufficient evidence" rather than guessing.
No personality-based assessments. Not "she's a natural leader" — instead: "she facilitated
the cross-team alignment meeting in Q3 and drove consensus on the API contract in 2 sessions."

---

## Step 3: Bias check

Before producing the assessment, check for:

| Bias | Check |
|------|-------|
| **Horns / Halo** | Is one strong trait (positive or negative) coloring your view of all other traits? Does each trait have its own independent evidence? |
| **Recency** | Are you only drawing from recent work? Check for evidence across the full period. |
| **Similarity** | Are you rating traits higher because the employee approaches work the way you would? Focus on outcomes. |
| **Central tendency** | Are you marking everything as "Emerging" to avoid differentiation? Commit to the evidence. |

If any bias is detected, revisit the affected traits and adjust.

---

## Step 4: Produce the assessment

### Strengths (2-4 traits)

For each strength:
- Name the trait/competency
- Provide 1-2 specific examples with outcomes
- If demonstrated above current level, note it explicitly
- Quote the framework's language for this trait at the relevant level (if available)

> **Example:**
> **Collaboration** — Consistently demonstrated (Senior Engineer level)
>
> Led the cross-team integration effort with the Platform team in Q3, coordinating
> 3 workstreams across 8 engineers. Proactively identified a dependency conflict in
> week 2 that would have delayed the release by 3 weeks, and facilitated a technical
> design session that resolved it.
>
> Framework expectation: *"Drive alignment across teams on shared technical decisions."*

### Growth indicators (traits above current level)

List any traits where the employee is operating at the next level. For promotion cases,
this section is critical — it demonstrates readiness.

For each:
- Name the trait and the level it corresponds to
- Provide the specific evidence
- Note whether this is consistent or a one-time demonstration

### Development areas (2-3 traits)

For each development area:
- Name the trait/competency
- State what the framework expects at this level
- Describe the specific gap — what's missing or inconsistent
- Be constructive: frame as "not yet" rather than "not capable"

> **Example:**
> **Communication — Stakeholder management** — Emerging
>
> Framework expects: *"Communicate project status and risks to stakeholders proactively."*
>
> Current state: Status updates are thorough when requested, but not proactively shared.
> The Q2 API delay was flagged to the PM only after the original deadline passed.
>
> Suggested focus: Establish a weekly async update cadence with key stakeholders.

### Development plan

For each development area, suggest:
- 1-2 concrete actions (not "improve communication" — instead: "present at the monthly architecture review twice in Q1")
- Opportunities: stretch projects, mentoring relationships, training, or exposure
- How to measure progress (what would "demonstrated" look like?)

### Promotion readiness (if applicable)

If this is a promotion case:
- Summary of traits at or above next level
- Summary of remaining gaps
- Honest assessment: Ready now / Ready with focused development / Not yet ready
- What needs to happen before promotion — be specific

## Output format

```
# Career Growth Assessment: [Employee Name]
**Current Level:** [Level] | **Purpose:** [Review prep / Promotion case / Development plan / Check-in]
**Assessment Date:** [Date] | **Period Covered:** [Period]

## Framework Used
[Name of framework or "General industry framework"]

## Strengths
[2-4 traits with evidence]

## Growth Indicators
[Traits demonstrated above current level, if any]

## Development Areas
[2-3 traits with specific gaps and framework expectations]

## Development Plan
[Concrete actions per development area]

## Promotion Readiness *(if applicable)*
[Summary assessment with evidence]
```

---

## Tone

- Developmental, not judgmental. This is a tool for growth.
- Specific and evidence-based. No vague praise or vague criticism.
- Honest about gaps. Sugarcoating development areas helps no one.
- Respectful of the employee — they may read this. Write it so they'd recognize their own work in it.
- Direct about promotion readiness. If they're not ready, say so clearly and say what would change that.
