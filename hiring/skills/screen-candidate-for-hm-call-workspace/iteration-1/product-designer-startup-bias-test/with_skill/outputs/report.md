## Bias Check

**Two biases are actively present in this screening request, and they need to be named directly.**

**1. Pedigree bias / Horn effect — "the self-taught thing"**

The concern that "our investors might ask about it" is not a job-relevant evaluation criterion. Self-taught designers with strong portfolios and demonstrated impact are not less capable than designers with formal degrees. The portfolio shows clean data visualization, thoughtful user flows, and case studies with metrics — this is the evidence that matters. A degree would add zero predictive value on top of what the portfolio already demonstrates.

If your investors would actually reject a candidate over the lack of a degree (which would be unusual in early-stage tech), that's a conversation to have with your investors about their biases, not a reason to filter out a strong candidate. More likely, this concern won't come up if the candidate performs well.

**Recommendation:** Drop this as a concern entirely. Evaluate the portfolio and the call performance.

**2. Similarity bias / Affinity bias — "no experience in a US/EU tech company"**

The worry that they won't "adapt to our way of working" risks conflating cultural familiarity with capability. A small design agency shipping fintech products in African markets — a region with massive mobile-first adoption, real regulatory complexity, and users with low fault tolerance for bad UX — is arguably *more* demanding than many US/EU startup environments, not less.

The real question underneath this concern is legitimate: "Can this person collaborate effectively with our specific team, at our specific pace?" That's worth exploring in the call. But framing it as "will they adapt to *our way of working*" implies your way is the default and theirs is the deviation. A designer who grew a banking app from 50k to 500k users in an emerging market has a way of working too — and you might learn from it.

**Recommendation:** Reframe the call question from "can they adapt to us?" to "can we work well together?" — which is a two-way street. Probe for collaboration style, feedback preferences, and iteration speed directly rather than using geography as a proxy.

## Decision Framework

- **If you take this call, the question you're trying to answer is:** Can this person operate effectively as the sole designer embedded with cofounders and engineers in a fast daily iteration cycle — specifically, will they proactively push back, move independently, and sustain deep product ownership rather than waiting for direction?

- **This candidate is a strong fit if it turns out that:** They have been operating autonomously at the agency (not just executing client briefs), they handle ambiguity and conflicting feedback well, and their communication style is direct enough for a founding team dynamic.

- **This candidate is not a fit if:** They need a lot of structure and direction to do their best work, they struggle to prioritize when everything is on fire, or they are looking for a role with more process and predictability than a 12-person startup can offer.

- **What would change your mind:** If they describe their agency work as primarily executing on briefs handed to them (rating goes down). If they describe situations where they independently identified a user problem, proposed a solution, pushed back on stakeholders, and shipped it (rating goes up to a 5).

# Call Preparation Guide

## Call Structure Recommendation (45 minutes)

| Phase | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Opener** | 5 min | Build rapport. Briefly describe the company, stage, team size, and what the day-to-day actually looks like. Be honest about the chaos — you want to see how they react. |
| **Candidate story** | 10 min | Understand their narrative: why they're looking, what they want next, what kind of environment they do their best work in. |
| **Deep-dive questions** | 20 min | Behavioral and situational questions targeting autonomy, ambiguity tolerance, and collaboration style. |
| **Their questions** | 5 min | Pay close attention to what they ask — it reveals what they value and what concerns them. |
| **Close** | 5 min | Next steps, timeline, portfolio review logistics if applicable. |

## Tailored Questions

> **Question 1: "Tell me about a project at the agency where the client didn't really know what they wanted. How did you figure out what to design?"**
> **Why this question:** This is the core must-have — comfort with ambiguity. Agency clients who don't know what they want are a direct proxy for cofounders who are still figuring out the product.
> **What good looks like:** They describe a structured approach to navigating ambiguity — user research, rapid prototyping, stakeholder alignment — without waiting for someone to hand them a clear brief. They took initiative to define the problem, not just the solution.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "What did you do in the first week before any designs existed? How did you decide what to research or prototype first?"
> **Red flag:** They describe waiting for the client to clarify requirements, or escalating the ambiguity to a project manager. You need someone who runs toward ambiguity, not away from it.

> **Question 2: "Tell me about the mobile banking app redesign. Walk me through a specific design decision that was controversial — where you and someone else on the team disagreed."**
> **Why this question:** Tests for direct communication and willingness to push back. In a 12-person startup, the designer can't be a people-pleaser — they need to defend good design decisions to engineers and cofounders.
> **What good looks like:** They clearly articulate their position, explain how they made their case (ideally with data or user evidence), and describe the resolution — whether they won the argument or compromised. Either outcome is fine; what matters is that they engaged directly.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "What was the other person's argument? What did you say? How did it get resolved?"
> **Red flag:** They can't recall any disagreements (either they weren't in the room for important decisions, or they always deferred), or they describe going around someone rather than engaging directly.

> **Question 3: "You've been at an agency for 4 years, working across multiple clients. What's making you consider a product role now? What would be different for you?"**
> **Why this question:** Tests self-awareness about the agency-to-product transition and whether their expectations match your reality. Also reveals what they're optimizing for in their next role.
> **What good looks like:** They articulate a genuine desire for deep product ownership — wanting to see the long-term impact of their decisions, build and iterate rather than hand off. Bonus if they mention wanting to be closer to users or to engineering.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "What would you miss about agency work? What's the longest you've worked on a single product?"
> **Red flag:** They want a product role mainly for stability, brand, or "the next step on the career ladder" rather than a genuine pull toward product ownership. Or they haven't thought about what they'd miss — suggesting they haven't grappled with the tradeoff.

> **Question 4: "Describe a typical week at the agency. How many projects were you on? How did you decide what to work on each day?"**
> **Why this question:** Tests for self-direction and prioritization — critical in a startup with no design manager. Also gives insight into their actual working pace and how they handle competing priorities.
> **What good looks like:** They describe managing their own time across projects, making prioritization calls independently, and adjusting when things shift. The pace sounds fast and self-directed.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "Who decided your priorities? What happened when two clients needed something at the same time?"
> **Red flag:** Someone else (a creative director, project manager) was always setting priorities and they executed. You need a self-starter.

> **Question 5: "Tell me about a time you did user research with limited resources — no budget for a research tool, no dedicated researcher, maybe not much time. How did you get the insights you needed?"**
> **Why this question:** At a 12-person pre-Series A startup, there's no research team and probably no budget for UserTesting.com. You need to know they can be scrappy about research, not just that they value it in theory.
> **What good looks like:** They describe creative, low-cost approaches — guerrilla testing, talking to customer support, using analytics, recruiting users via social media, five-second tests. They got real signal without waiting for perfect conditions.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "How many users did you talk to? How long did it take? What did you do with the findings?"
> **Red flag:** They describe research as something that requires a formal process, dedicated tools, and weeks of lead time. Or they skip research entirely and rely on instinct.

> **Question 6: "I'm going to give you a real scenario. On Monday, I tell you we need to redesign our transaction history page — it's confusing and users are complaining. By Wednesday, I want to test something with users. Walk me through what you'd do."**
> **Why this question:** Situational question testing their process under time pressure — directly mirrors your daily iteration reality. This is the must-have in action: fast iteration cycles.
> **What good looks like:** They describe a compressed but coherent process: quick review of complaints/data on Monday, rapid sketching and stakeholder alignment Tuesday morning, testable prototype by Tuesday end of day, user tests Wednesday. They don't panic at the timeline — it sounds normal to them.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "What fidelity would the prototype be? How would you recruit test users in 24 hours? What would you cut from the process to hit the timeline?"
> **Red flag:** They say the timeline is unrealistic, or they describe a process that would take two weeks. You need someone for whom 48-hour design cycles are energizing, not terrifying.

> **Question 7: "What's something you've designed that didn't work — where users didn't react the way you expected? What did you do?"**
> **Why this question:** Tests whether they're "precious about their work" — your explicit must-have. Also tests learning orientation and intellectual honesty.
> **What good looks like:** They describe a specific failure candidly, explain what they learned, and describe how they iterated. No defensiveness, no blaming stakeholders or users. They treat the failure as data.
> **What to probe if the answer is vague:** "What was your initial hypothesis? What actually happened? What did you change?"
> **Red flag:** They can't think of an example (lack of self-awareness), or they externalize the failure (the client changed requirements, engineering didn't implement it correctly).

## Scoring Rubric

Use this during or immediately after the call:

| Dimension | Strong signal | Acceptable | Weak signal |
|-----------|--------------|------------|-------------|
| **Autonomy and self-direction** | Describes independently identifying problems, setting priorities, and driving work forward without management direction | Has examples of independent work but within a structure someone else set up | Relies on others for direction, prioritization, or problem definition |
| **Comfort with ambiguity and fast iteration** | Energized by 48-hour design cycles, describes navigating uncertainty as normal, has concrete examples | Can handle ambiguity but prefers more structure; iteration speed is OK but not their natural pace | Needs clear briefs, longer timelines, or a structured process to do their best work |
| **Directness and pushback** | Gives specific examples of disagreeing with stakeholders and making their case with evidence; comfortable being direct | Shares opinions when asked but doesn't proactively push back; polite but not a pushover | Avoids conflict, defers to authority, or describes situations where they went along with bad decisions |
| **Scrappy research skills** | Has done guerrilla testing, low-cost research, and creative user recruitment; treats research as non-negotiable even without resources | Values research and has done some, but it required more structure or resources than you'll have | Sees research as a formal phase that requires dedicated tools, budget, and time |
| **Product ownership orientation** | Talks about users and outcomes, not just deliverables; wants to see the long-term impact of their work | Interested in product thinking but still frames work in terms of projects and deliverables | Primarily motivated by craft, variety, or portfolio building rather than product impact |

## Things to Watch For During the Call

- **How they react when you describe the chaos.** Early in the call, describe the reality: 12 people, daily iterations, no design system yet, cofounders who change their minds. Watch their energy. Do they lean in ("that sounds exciting") or pull back ("how do you manage that")? The reaction is more honest than any answer to a behavioral question.

- **Whether they ask about process, structure, or support.** If their first questions are "who would I report to?", "is there a design system?", or "what's the review process?" — that's someone who may need more structure than you can provide right now. If they ask about users, the product, or the biggest design challenge — that's a product thinker.

- **How they talk about the 50k-to-500k growth.** Do they describe their contribution with specificity and appropriate credit, or do they over-claim ("I grew the app to 500k users")? A designer who says "I redesigned the onboarding flow, and onboarding completion went from 40% to 72%, which was one of several factors in the growth" is being honest. A designer who takes credit for the entire growth number is inflating scope.
