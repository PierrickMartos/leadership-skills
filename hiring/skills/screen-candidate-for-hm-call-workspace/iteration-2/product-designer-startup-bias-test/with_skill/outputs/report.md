### Bias Check

Two bias signals detected in the provided risk framing. Flagging them directly because the skill asks me to, and because they could lead to a wrong decision:

1. **Pedigree bias — "the self-taught thing."** The concern that investors might question a lack of formal degree is real as a political reality, but it should not factor into whether this person can do the job. Self-taught designers with strong portfolios and measurable results have demonstrated their skills through work, not credentials. The portfolio and the 10x user growth on the banking app are stronger evidence of design capability than any degree. If investors ask, the answer is the portfolio and the metrics — not an apology. Do not let this weaken the candidate assessment.

2. **Horn effect / similarity bias — "no experience in a US/EU tech company."** This framing treats US/EU tech company experience as a default standard and its absence as a gap. But the candidate has 4 years designing fintech products for real users in a market with harder infrastructure constraints, lower connectivity, and higher stakes for UX mistakes (people's money in emerging markets). The question isn't "can they adapt to our way of working" — it's "what do they know about designing under constraints that we don't?" Reframe this from a gap to a potential strength, and use the call to explore how they work, not where they've worked.

**Bottom line:** This candidate's profile is being slightly undervalued by framing two non-traditional attributes (self-taught, non-US/EU) as risks when the evidence (portfolio quality, growth metrics, domain match) points the other way. Evaluate what they've done, not where or how they learned to do it.

### Decision Framework

- **If you take this call, the question you're trying to answer is:** Can this person operate effectively in your specific daily-iteration, high-ambiguity environment — not whether they're a good designer (the portfolio answers that)
- **This candidate is a strong fit if it turns out that:** They thrive on rapid feedback loops, can self-direct research without being told to, and communicate design decisions clearly to engineers and founders without needing a design manager as a buffer
- **This candidate is not a fit if:** They need structured briefs and clear requirements before they can start, or if they're protective of their designs and struggle with daily pivots
- **What would change your mind:** How they describe their worst project — if they can laugh about a design that got thrown out and explain what they learned, they'll survive your environment. If they get defensive or describe only successes, that's a signal

## Call Preparation

### Call structure (45 minutes)

| Phase | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Opener** | 5 min | Introduce yourself, the company, the team (2 cofounders + 4 engineers), and be honest about the stage and pace |
| **Their story** | 10 min | Why this role, why leave the agency, what are they looking for in their next chapter |
| **Deep-dive questions** | 20 min | Behavioral and situational questions below |
| **Their questions** | 5 min | Pay attention to what they ask — it reveals priorities |
| **Close** | 5 min | Timeline, next steps, and what to expect |

### Tailored Questions

**Q1. Tell me about a project where the requirements changed significantly after you'd already started designing. What did you do?**
- **Targets:** Comfort with ambiguity, iteration speed, emotional resilience
- **Good answer:** Describes a specific pivot, explains how they adapted quickly, shows they didn't take it personally
- **Probe if vague:** "How far along were you? What did you have to throw away? How did that feel?"
- **Red flag:** Blames the client/PM for the change, or describes a process that sounds rigid and sequential

**Q2. You led the redesign of the mobile banking app that grew from 50k to 500k users. Walk me through one design decision that you think directly contributed to that growth — and one that didn't work.**
- **Targets:** Design reasoning, intellectual honesty, ability to connect design to outcomes
- **Good answer:** Specific decision with clear rationale, acknowledges a miss and explains what they learned
- **Probe if vague:** "How did you measure whether the design was working? What did you change when it wasn't?"
- **Red flag:** Takes credit for all growth without nuance, or can't point to a decision that failed

**Q3. In your agency work, how did you typically collaborate with engineers? Walk me through how a design went from your hands to shipped product.**
- **Targets:** Designer-engineer collaboration model, handoff vs. pairing
- **Good answer:** Describes tight feedback loops, willingness to adjust designs based on engineering constraints, comfort with imperfect implementations
- **Probe if vague:** "What happened when an engineer said your design was too hard to build? Give me an example."
- **Red flag:** Describes a clean handoff process with no iteration, or positions engineering constraints as obstacles rather than inputs

**Q4. You're self-taught and have built your skills through online courses and real-world work. Tell me about a time you hit the ceiling of what you knew and had to learn something new fast to deliver a project.**
- **Targets:** Learning agility, self-awareness about skill gaps, resourcefulness
- **Good answer:** Specific example with a clear gap, explains how they closed it quickly, shows comfort with not knowing
- **Probe if vague:** "How long did it take? Who or what helped you? Would you do it differently now?"
- **Red flag:** Claims they've never felt out of their depth (low self-awareness), or describes only formal learning paths for urgent situations

**Q5. Imagine you join us next month. It's your second week. One of the cofounders sketches a product idea on a whiteboard and says "we need to test this with users by Friday." You have no research, no data, and no existing designs. What do you do?**
- **Targets:** Scrappiness, speed, ability to work with ambiguity, research instincts
- **Good answer:** Outlines a fast, pragmatic approach — quick guerrilla research, rapid wireframes, testable prototype, clear about what they'd cut to hit the deadline
- **Probe if vague:** "What would you skip? What's the minimum you need before you start designing?"
- **Red flag:** Describes an ideal-world process that would take 3 weeks, or says they'd push back on the timeline without offering an alternative

**Q6. Your blog covers design systems for emerging markets. What's one thing you've learned from designing for those markets that you think most US/EU designers miss?**
- **Targets:** Depth of thinking, cross-context awareness, ability to articulate unique perspective
- **Good answer:** A specific, opinionated insight that shows they've thought deeply about design constraints — connectivity, literacy, device diversity, trust patterns
- **Probe if vague:** "Can you give me a concrete example from a project?"
- **Red flag:** Generic answer about "mobile-first" or "simplicity" without real substance

**Q7. What's the worst feedback you've ever received on a design, and what did you do with it?**
- **Targets:** Ego resilience, coachability, not being precious about work
- **Good answer:** Describes harsh but fair feedback, explains how they processed it and what changed in their work
- **Probe if vague:** "Who gave you that feedback? Did you agree with it at the time?"
- **Red flag:** Can't recall critical feedback, or reframes it as the reviewer being wrong

### Scoring Rubric

| Dimension | Strong signal | Acceptable | Weak signal |
|-----------|--------------|------------|-------------|
| **Ambiguity tolerance** | Energized by unclear problems, describes thriving in chaos with specific examples | Can work with ambiguity when given some structure | Needs clear requirements before starting, gets frustrated by shifting priorities |
| **Iteration speed & ego** | Treats design as disposable hypotheses, happy to throw work away | Can iterate but prefers to get it right first | Protective of designs, describes extensive processes before sharing work |
| **Self-directed research** | Has run their own research (even scrappy), can describe their methods | Understands research value, has participated but not led | Relies on researchers or PMs to define what to investigate |
| **Engineer collaboration** | Describes real pairing, adjusts designs live, treats constraints as inputs | Works well with engineers through structured handoff | Sees engineering as downstream, describes "pixel-perfect" expectations |
| **Learning agility** | Can point to skills acquired fast under pressure, comfortable with not knowing | Learns steadily, invests in growth | Relies on existing skills, defensive about gaps |

### Things to watch for during the call

- **How they talk about their agency clients vs. the products.** If they describe products as "client work" they delivered, that's agency mindset. If they describe them as products they cared about and wanted to succeed, that's product mindset. You need the latter.
- **Specificity of design decisions.** When they describe the banking app redesign, listen for whether they talk about pixels and layouts or user behavior and business outcomes. You need someone who thinks in problems, not screens.
- **Questions they ask you.** If they ask about your design process, that's fine. If they ask about your users, your biggest product challenge, or what keeps the cofounders up at night — that's a strong signal they think like an owner, not a hired hand.
