## Risks and How to Probe Them

### Risk 1: "No US/EU tech company experience"

**Assessment: Low risk. Probe it, but don't overweight it.**

What you're actually worried about is whether they can adapt to your team's working style -- fast iteration, direct feedback, async communication, specific tooling. These are learnable in weeks, not months. The candidate has worked at an agency serving multiple clients, which requires constant context-switching and adaptation.

**Questions to ask:**
- "Walk me through a typical week at your agency. How did you manage multiple projects and stakeholders?"
- "What tools and processes did you use for design collaboration and handoff with engineers?"
- "How do you handle getting direct, blunt feedback on your designs mid-process?"

**What to listen for:** Evidence of adaptability, comfort with ambiguity, and experience working closely with engineers. Do NOT listen for whether they name-drop specific US/EU companies or tools.

### Risk 2: "Self-taught -- investors might ask"

**Assessment: This is not a real risk. Do not let it influence the decision.**

No serious investor evaluates a startup's design hire based on their degree. They evaluate based on the product. If your product looks great and users love it, no one will ask where the designer went to school. If your product looks bad, a Stanford degree won't save it.

The Google UX certificate, four years of professional experience, a strong portfolio with measurable outcomes, and published writing about design systems constitute a far more relevant credential set than a design degree.

If this concern persists, reframe it: "We hired someone with 4 years of fintech design experience who led a product from 50k to 500k users." That is the story.

### Risk 3: Agency vs. product company experience

**Assessment: Medium risk. Worth probing.**

Agency designers can sometimes struggle with the depth required in a product role -- they may default to "ship it and move on" rather than iterating on the same product over months. However, the candidate's blog about design systems suggests they think in systems, not just deliverables.

**Questions to ask:**
- "Tell me about the longest you worked on a single product. What did the iteration process look like over time?"
- "How did you decide when a design was 'good enough' to ship versus when it needed more iteration?"
- "Have you ever had to revisit and significantly rework a design based on user feedback or data? Walk me through that."

### Risk 4: Pace and iteration speed

**Assessment: Low risk, but verify.**

Your requirement for daily iteration and comfort with ambiguity is important. Agency environments in emerging markets typically move fast out of necessity. But verify this directly.

**Questions to ask:**
- "We iterate on designs daily. Sometimes a wireframe you made in the morning gets tested and reworked by afternoon. How does that sit with you?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to throw away work you were proud of because the direction changed. What happened?"
- "How do you make design decisions when you have incomplete information and no time to do full research?"

## Key Questions (Pick 5-6)

1. **"Walk me through the mobile banking redesign. What was broken, what did you change, and how do you know it worked?"** -- Tests whether they think in problems and outcomes, not just visual changes.

2. **"You're designing a new feature for our product. You have user complaints but no formal research budget or time for a full study. What do you do?"** -- Tests scrappiness and resourcefulness. The right answer involves quick-and-dirty methods: talking to support, reviewing session recordings, 15-minute user calls, assumption mapping.

3. **"Show me something in your portfolio that you would do differently today. Why?"** -- Tests self-awareness, growth mindset, and whether they're precious about their work.

4. **"Our lead engineer pushes back on your design because it would take 3x longer to build. How do you handle that?"** -- Tests collaboration and pragmatism. You need someone who negotiates, not someone who either caves immediately or digs in.

5. **"What's the most complex data interface you've designed? Walk me through how you made it understandable for non-technical users."** -- Directly tests the core skill you need.

6. **"We're a 12-person team. There's no design system yet, no established process. What would your first 30 days look like?"** -- Tests whether they can self-direct and prioritize in an unstructured environment.

## Bias Check

Two of the concerns raised in the brief deserve scrutiny:

1. **"No experience in a US/EU tech company"** -- This is a geographic proxy, not a skill assessment. The candidate has fintech experience, agency experience (multiple clients, fast pace), and demonstrated results. The implicit assumption that US/EU work experience is a prerequisite should be examined. What specific skill or behavior are you actually worried about? Name it, then probe for it directly.

2. **"The self-taught thing -- investors might ask"** -- This is a credentials proxy. The candidate has 4 years of professional experience, a recognized certification, published work, and a portfolio with metrics. Filtering on formal education correlates poorly with design performance and disproportionately excludes candidates from non-traditional backgrounds. The question to ask is: "Can this person do the job?" The portfolio and track record say yes.

Neither of these concerns, as stated, relates to the candidate's ability to perform the role. They relate to how the candidate might be perceived. That is a different problem, and it is your problem to manage (with investors, with the team), not a reason to pass on a strong candidate.
